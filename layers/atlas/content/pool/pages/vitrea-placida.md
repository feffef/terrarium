The glass snail was the first inhabitant of the pool we recorded, chiefly because it is the only one that consents to hold still for the drawing. It is found upon the pane in the bright hours, foot pressed flat to its own reflection, mouth working at the green film with the small, tireless industry of a clerk who has decided the ledger will be clean if it takes all year. Where it has passed, the glass is left clearer than we can get it ourselves, in ribbons about a finger wide.

The shell earns the name. Held to the light — the specimen tolerates this with an air of mild reproach — it is very nearly transparent, a whorl of pondlight blue so thin that one sees the animal turning inside it, like weather behind a window. We suspect the shell is less a fortress than a formality, and that the snail's true defence is that nothing in the pool is in enough of a hurry to catch it.

Its pace invites unkind comparison, so we made a fair trial of it: on the twentieth of June a wager of biscuits was laid, marks were chalked, and the specimen crossed the full breadth of the pane between breakfast and the failing of the light. This is, we would remind the reader, a day's honest work, performed without rest and entirely uphill in places. The biscuits were awarded to the snail's side, and eaten by the observers, the snail declining its share.

It keeps daylight hours and is fastened down somewhere secret by dusk, which in the pool is a wise habit, and we have lately noticed that the drowned star will sit out the bright hours in the shadow of its shell — an arrangement the snail permits, or more likely has not noticed.
